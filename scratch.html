A Brief History of Golf

Golf is one of the oldest sports in the world, with roots stretching back to 15th-century Scotland. The game was reportedly played on the linksland of St Andrews as early as 1457, when King James II of Scotland tried to ban it because it was distracting soldiers from archery practice. The ban didn't stick — and thankfully so.

The modern game as we know it took shape in the 18th and 19th centuries. The Royal and Ancient Golf Club of St Andrews was founded in 1754, and the first formal rules were codified shortly after. The sport spread to England, then across the British Empire, eventually reaching the United States by the late 1800s.

Today, golf is played in over 200 countries with more than 30,000 courses worldwide. It is one of the few sports with origins traceable to a single region and a continuous 500-year history.

How to Play Golf

Golf is elegantly simple in concept but endlessly complex in execution. The goal: get the ball in the hole in as few strokes as possible.

  1. The Basics
    • A standard round consists of 18 holes
    • Each hole has a "par" — the expected number of strokes for a skilled golfer
    • Pars are typically 3, 4, or 5 depending on hole length
    • Scoring: Birdie = 1 under par, Eagle = 2 under par, Bogey = 1 over par
  2. The Equipment
    • Players carry up to 14 clubs in their bag
    • Woods and hybrids for long shots off the tee
    • Irons (2–9) for approach shots to the green
    • Wedges for short game and sand shots
    • Putter — used on the green to roll the ball into the hole
  3. Course Zones
    • Tee Box — where each hole begins
    • Fairway — the manicured grass between tee and green
    • Rough — longer grass bordering the fairway
    • Bunkers — sand traps that penalize errant shots
    • Green — the putting surface surrounding the hole
  4. Etiquette & Rules
    • Play at a reasonable pace — 4 hours for 18 holes is the standard
    • Repair ball marks on the green and replace divots in the fairway
    • Stay quiet while others are hitting
    • The player farthest from the hole plays first

Famous Courses Around the World

Part of what makes golf so special is its venues. From windswept coastal links to manicured parkland courses, no two golf courses are alike. Here are some of the most iconic:

🏭 Augusta National

Home of The Masters, Augusta is perhaps the most famous golf course in the world. Designed by Bobby Jones and Alister MacKenzie, it's known for its pristine conditions, azalea blooms, and Amen Corner.

18 holes  |  Par 72

🏭 Pebble Beach Golf Links

Perched on the cliffs of Monterey Bay in California, Pebble Beach is famous for its stunning ocean views and has hosted multiple U.S. Opens. One of the few public courses on this list.

18 holes  |  Par 72

🏭 St Andrews Old Course

The "Home of Golf" in Scotland. The Old Course dates back centuries and features the famous Swilcan Bridge. Hosting the Open Championship, it is a pilgrimage destination for every golfer.

18 holes  |  Par 72

🏭 Bonneville Golf Course

My home course right here in Salt Lake City, Utah. Nestled in the foothills of the Wasatch Mountains, Bonneville is a public course with beautiful views and challenging terrain. This is where I first fell in love with golf.

18 holes  |  Par 72

Why I Love Golf

I picked up a golf club for the first time when I was 8 years old, dragged along by my dad on an early Saturday morning at Bonneville. I didn't want to be there. By the 9th hole, I never wanted to leave.

Golf is unique among sports — it is simultaneously the most humbling and the most rewarding game you can play. A single perfect shot, struck purely with a 7-iron, can undo three holes of frustration. No caddy. No teammates. Just you, the course, and a decision on every single shot.

I played varsity golf in high school and still try to squeeze in rounds whenever my schedule allows. My handicap index is currently +4.2. My personal best round is a 67 at Bonneville Golf Course.
